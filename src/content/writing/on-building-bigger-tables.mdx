There's a José Andrés quote I keep coming back to: *"The world eats better when there are more cooks."*

He meant it literally — he was talking about World Central Kitchen, about training local cooks in disaster zones, about the philosophy behind his entire approach to humanitarian food work. But every time I hear it, I can't help reading it as product management advice.

---

I started volunteering in professional kitchens a few years ago. Nothing glamorous — prep work, mostly. Cutting vegetables. Washing dishes. Occasionally manning a station on a busy service night.

What I learned there changed how I do my job.

**Kitchens don't work without systems.** Every great kitchen runs on mise en place — everything in its place, everything prepared before service starts. A chaotic kitchen makes bad food slowly. A disciplined one makes great food fast. Product teams are the same. The work you do before sprint planning determines what happens during it.

**You cook for the person eating, not for yourself.** The most common mistake I see from new cooks — and new PMs — is centering their own perspective. What *they* think is elegant. What *they* think is impressive. A great dish disappears into the person eating it. A great feature feels inevitable to the user.

**Someone has to do the dishes.** Every glamorous product launch has unglamorous work underneath it. The PM who only wants to be in the room for strategy but not for QA, not for the 6am incident call, not for the thank-you email to the team — that PM doesn't last.

---

I titled this site "Building bigger tables" because I believe that's the job — in product, in volunteering, in being a person in a community. Not hoarding seats. Not gatekeeping. Building a table that fits more people and making sure everyone at it is fed.

It's not a complicated philosophy. It's just harder to practice than to say.

The kitchen taught me that.
