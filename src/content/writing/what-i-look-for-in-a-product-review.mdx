Product reviews have a bad reputation. Teams dread them. Leaders dominate them. Decisions made in them get relitigated in Slack two days later.

I've run a lot of product reviews. I've been in many more. Here's what I've learned separates the useful ones from the theater.

## The review is not a status update

If the first 20 minutes of your product review are a walk-through of what the team has been doing, you've already lost the room. Leaders don't need a recitation — they need to be useful. Get to the question you actually need them to answer.

A useful framing: *"We're here because we have a decision to make. Here's the decision, here's the context, here's our recommendation. Do you agree?"*

## Disagreement is data

The worst thing that can happen in a product review isn't pushback. It's everyone nodding. If a room full of smart people with different context all immediately agree with your proposal, either you've buried the real question or nobody is paying attention.

Seek out the dissent. Ask the quiet people what they think. Invite the skeptic to poke holes. The decision you make after genuine challenge is almost always better than the one made under comfortable consensus.

## The loudest person in the room is often the least certain

This is just pattern recognition. The leader who hedges, who asks more questions than they answer, who asks to see it again before deciding — they're usually doing the real work. The one who arrives with a strong opinion immediately and never updates it is usually the one you'll be cleaning up after.

## Leave with one decision, not ten

Every product review should end with one clear answer to one clear question. Not a list of follow-ups. Not "let's schedule another review." One decision, who owns it, and what happens next.

If you can't get to that, the review wasn't ready to happen.

---

The format isn't what makes a product review useful. The clarity of thinking that goes into it is. That's on the PM.
